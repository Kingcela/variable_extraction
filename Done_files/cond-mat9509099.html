Cooperative chiral order plays a vital role in the self-assembly of ordered supramolecular structures in liquid crystalsÂ [1], organic thin filmsÂ [2, 3, 4, 5], and lipid membranesÂ [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. One particularly simple and well-controlled example of cooperative chiral order is in random copolymers. Recent experiments have found that polyisocyanates formed from a mixture of opposite enantiomers follow a chiral â€œmajority ruleâ€Â [13]. The chiral order of the copolymer, measured by optical activity, responds sharply to slight differences in the concentrations of the enantiomers, and is dominated by whichever enantiomer is in the majority. In this paper, we show that the majority rule can be understood through a mapping of the random copolymer onto the random-field Ising modelÂ [14, 15, 16]. Using this model, we predict the chiral order as a function of enantiomer concentration, in quantitative agreement with the experiments, and show that the sharpness of the majority-rule curve is determined by two energy scales associated with the chiral packing of monomers.

In a series of experiments, Green et al.Â have investigated chiral order in polyisocyanatesÂ [17]. This polymer consists of a carbon-nitrogen backbone with a pendant group attached to each monomer, as shown in Fig.Â 1. Although the backbone is nonchiral, steric constraints force the molecule to polymerize in a helical structure. If the pendant group is also nonchiral, the helix is randomly right- or left-handed. A long chain then consists of domains of fixed helicity, separated by occasional helix reversals. On average, there are equal right- and left-handed domains, leading to zero net optical activity. However, if the pendant group is chiral, there is a preference for one sense of the helix, which leads to a net optical activity. Because of the cooperative interaction between the monomers in a domain, even a very small chiral influence leads to a large optical activityÂ [18]. Most recently, Green et al.Â have synthesized random copolymers with a mixture of right- and left-handed enantiomeric pendant groups, with concentrations pğ‘italic-pp and 1-p1ğ‘NUMBER:1 ADDOP:minus italic-p1-p, respectivelyÂ [13]. To explain this cooperative chiral order theoretically, we map the random copolymer onto the one-dimensional random-field Ising model, a standard model in the theory of random magnetic systemsÂ [14, 15, 16]. Although related models have been applied to other polymer systemsÂ [19, 20, 21, 22], our theory gives a new, direct correspondence between the Ising order parameter and the optical activity. This correspondence provides a novel experimental test of predictions for the random-field Ising model. Finally, we note that the sharp majority-rule curve in polyisocyanates can be exploited in an optical switchÂ [24]. If a mixture of enantiomers is exposed to a pulse of circularly polarized light, one enantiomer is preferentially excited into a higher-energy state. That state can decay into either chiral form. Thus, a light pulse depletes the preferentially excited enantiomer and changes the enantiomer concentration pğ‘italic-pp, which changes the optical activity. In other systems, this approach has been limited by the fact that light pulses induce only a slight change in pğ‘italic-pp, and hence only a slight change in optical activity. However, in polyisocyanates, a slight change in pğ‘italic-pp close to p=12ğ‘12italic-p RELOP:equals FRACOP:italic-divide ARG:start NUMBER:1 ARG:end ARG:start NUMBER:2 ARG:endp=\frac{1}{2} is sufficient to induce a very significant change in optical activity. Our theory shows how to optimize the majority-rule curve for use in an optical switch.
